According to the report, international students contributed over **$40 billion to the U.S. economy** during the 2022-2023 academic year, supporting more than **368,000 jobs**. This financial impact extends far beyond tuition fees; students spend on housing, food, travel, and entertainment, enriching local communities across the nation.

## üéì Academic and Workforce Benefits

International students are not just consumers; they are integral to the academic and intellectual vibrancy of U.S. universities:

*   **STEM and Research Boost**: A significant number of international students enroll in **Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM) fields**, as well as doctoral-level programs. They fill vital roles in research labs, contribute to cutting-edge discoveries, and often co-author academic papers.
*   **Talent Pipeline**: Many international graduates choose to gain practical experience in the U.S. through programs like **Optional Practical Training (OPT)**. This allows them to work for 12 months (with a 24-month extension for STEM fields) in a job related to their studies. Some later transition to skilled worker visas like the H-1B, directly contributing to the U.S. workforce in high-demand sectors.
*   **Global Perspectives**: International students bring diverse viewpoints, cultural richness, and new ideas to U.S. campuses, enhancing the learning environment for all students, both domestic and international.

## üõÇ A Call for Smarter U.S. Policies to Attract Global Talent

Despite the immense value international students bring, the report notes that the U.S. is facing increasing competition for global talent from countries like **Canada, the UK, and Australia**. These nations are often perceived as having more straightforward and welcoming immigration pathways for students and graduates.

The report urges the U.S. to:
*   Streamline and improve the [student visa application process](/interview-qa#usa).
*   Expand and clarify policies regarding dual intent (the ability to intend to study temporarily but also potentially seek permanent residency later).
*   Enhance and make more predictable the pathways for international graduates to gain work experience and potentially transition to long-term employment or residency.
*   Address the long backlogs for employment-based Green Cards.

## üåç Why This Matters for Nepalese Students

This ongoing advocacy for more favorable policies could mean positive changes for Nepalese students considering the U.S.:
*   **Improved Visa Processes**: Efforts to make the visa application smoother and more transparent. This includes addressing the new [social media screening requirements](/blog/us-student-visa-social-media-screening).
*   **Enhanced Post-Study Opportunities**: Advocacy for clearer guidelines and possibly expanded opportunities for OPT and transitioning to work visas.
*   **Stronger Institutional Support**: U.S. universities are powerful advocates for international students and will continue pushing for policies that support their recruitment and retention.
